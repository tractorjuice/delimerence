## Summary

The UK Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA) is actively developing a new regulatory framework for AI in healthcare, expected to be published in 2026. The regulatory landscape for digital mental health technologies is in flux, with the boundary between "wellness tool" and "medical device" particularly ambiguous for AI-enabled chatbots that deliver therapeutic techniques. A tool's classification depends on its medical purpose and functional impact, with risk-based tiering from Class I (self-certification) through Class III (Notified Body assessment). The MHRA and NICE have established an AI airlock (regulatory sandbox) for testing new tools with the regulator before wider deployment.

## Key Findings

- **Risk-based classification model.** The MHRA uses functional impact to determine classification. Low-risk products (basic screening questionnaires, simple wellbeing apps) may fall outside medical device regulation or self-certify as Class I. Higher-risk tools -- including AI-enabled chatbots that contribute to diagnosis or treatment planning -- may be classified as Class IIa, IIb, or Class III, requiring Notified Body assessment.

- **Wellness vs medical device boundary.** A digital mental health tool with a medical purpose but low functional impact could be excluded from medical device regulation if it does not provide a clinical effect or influence patient care decisions. However, tools where a patient answers AI-tailored questions showing the likelihood of mental health conditions are considered high functionality. Tools with generative-AI chatbots that respond adaptively based on user input are also considered high functionality.

- **DeLimerence positioning challenge.** DeLimerence delivers CBT/ERP techniques (ABCDE cognitive restructuring, exposure and response prevention) and performs phase assessment (initiation, crystallisation, deterioration, resolution). This could be interpreted as treatment and clinical triage, potentially triggering Class IIa classification. The phased launch strategy (Phase 1: psychoeducation and ritual tracking only; Phase 2: CBT/ERP after MHRA clarity) is designed to de-risk this.

- **MHRA/NICE AI airlock.** The MHRA and NICE are establishing a digital mental health technology AI airlock -- a regulatory sandbox that allows companies to test new tools with the regulator before wider NHS rollout. This is directly relevant to DeLimerence and should be explored as a pathway for gaining regulatory clarity without committing to the full medical device pathway.

- **New guidance published in 2025.** The MHRA published guidance specifically addressing how digital mental health technologies should be assessed, qualified, and classified under UK medical device regulations, developed as part of a Wellcome Trust project with GBP 2 million in government funding.

- **National regulatory framework for AI in healthcare.** The MHRA and the National Commission into the Regulation of AI in Healthcare are developing a new national framework to be published in 2026. This will directly affect DeLimerence's regulatory pathway.

- **Parliamentary briefing on AI and mental healthcare.** The Parliamentary Office of Science and Technology (POST) published a briefing on ethical and regulatory considerations for AI in mental healthcare, indicating ongoing legislative attention to this space.
