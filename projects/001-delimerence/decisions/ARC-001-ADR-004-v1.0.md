## Context and Problem Statement

DeLimerence is a conversational AI tool that delivers CBT/ERP techniques and performs phase assessment for limerence recovery. The tool's clinical feature set -- ABCDE cognitive restructuring (FR-008), guided ERP for ritual resistance (FR-012), and four-phase limerence assessment (FR-003) -- could be interpreted by the UK Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA) as constituting treatment and clinical triage. This would potentially trigger medical device classification under UK MDR 2002, most likely Class IIa, which would require UKCA marking, Notified Body assessment, clinical evidence, a quality management system (ISO 13485), and post-market surveillance.

Classification as a medical device would add 12-18 months to the regulatory pathway and £50,000-100,000 in compliance costs, conflicting with the Q4 2026 public beta target (Goal G-1) and the regulatory compliance objective (Goal G-3). The MHRA is actively developing new AI healthcare regulations expected to be published in 2026, and the boundary between "wellness tool" and "medical device" remains genuinely ambiguous for AI-enabled digital mental health technologies. The MHRA and NICE have established an AI airlock (regulatory sandbox) for testing new tools with the regulator before wider deployment.

Risk R-002 (MHRA medical device classification) carries the highest residual risk score in the project risk register at 16 (Likelihood 4 x Impact 4), with controls assessed as preparatory rather than mitigating -- the classification outcome depends on an external regulatory decision that project controls cannot directly influence.

## Decision Drivers

* **Regulatory ambiguity**: The MHRA boundary between wellness tool and medical device is unclear for AI mental health apps that deliver therapeutic techniques adaptively [uk-mhra-digital-mental-health tech note]
* **Timeline constraint**: Q4 2026 public beta target (Goal G-1) is incompatible with 12-18 month medical device pathway
* **Cost constraint**: £50,000-100,000 regulatory costs for Class IIa certification significantly exceed the founder-led project budget (£275K 3-year TCO)
* **User need**: People experiencing limerence need access to clinically grounded tools at the point of crisis; delay costs real harm
* **Regulatory evolution**: MHRA is developing new AI healthcare regulations for 2026; early engagement shapes the tool's positioning before the regulatory framework crystallises
* **Clinical value preservation**: Phase 1 features (psychoeducation, ritual tracking, anti-dependency architecture) deliver genuine therapeutic value even without CBT/ERP modules
* **Precedent risk**: A competitor product classified as a device, or a user complaint to MHRA, could trigger investigation regardless of positioning

---

## Considered Options

1. **Do Nothing** -- Launch with full CBT/ERP features without MHRA engagement
2. **Phased Launch Strategy with Early MHRA Engagement** (chosen)
3. **Full Launch with Medical Device Pathway** -- Accept Class IIa classification and pursue UKCA marking
4. **Position as Wellness Tool Only** -- Strip all CBT/ERP features, provide psychoeducation content only

---

## Decision Outcome

**Chosen option: Option 2 -- Phased Launch Strategy with Early MHRA Engagement**, because it de-risks the regulatory pathway while still delivering genuine user value in Phase 1, preserves the Q4 2026 beta timeline for Phase 1 scope, and establishes a constructive regulatory relationship with MHRA using a lower-risk feature set as the basis for the pre-submission meeting.

### Y-Statement

In the context of launching a CBT/ERP-based AI tool for limerence recovery, facing ambiguous MHRA classification boundaries for digital mental health tools, we decided for a phased launch strategy with early MHRA engagement, to achieve regulatory de-risking while still delivering genuine user value in Phase 1, accepting deferred delivery of cognitive restructuring and guided ERP features.

### Implementation

**Phase 1 (Beta -- Q4 2026):**
- Psychoeducation content delivery (FR-005) -- informational, not treatment
- Ritual tracking with persistent cross-session storage (FR-004)
- Full anti-dependency architecture: session limits (FR-001), cooldown periods (FR-002), action commitment gate (FR-006), anti-warmth behaviour (FR-007), transparency/self-identification (FR-011)
- Phase assessment (FR-003) -- positioned as response calibration, not clinical diagnosis
- "Not a therapist" messaging embedded throughout UI and documentation (NFR-C-003)
- All Phase 1 features clearly below the MHRA device threshold

**Phase 2 (Post-MHRA Clarity):**
- ABCDE cognitive restructuring module (FR-008) -- deferred from MUST_HAVE to SHOULD_HAVE
- ERP ritual resistance support (FR-012) -- deferred from MUST_HAVE to SHOULD_HAVE
- Activation contingent on written MHRA classification determination

**MHRA Engagement:**
- Schedule MHRA Innovation Office pre-submission meeting by 2026-07-06
- Present Phase 1 scope for classification determination
- Prepare classification justification document with legal counsel
- Investigate MHRA/NICE AI airlock (regulatory sandbox) as pathway for gaining regulatory clarity without committing to full medical device certification
- Design Phase 2 features with self-help framing to minimise classification risk

### Consequences

**Good:**
- Phase 1 delivers genuine value -- psychoeducation, ritual tracking, and anti-dependency architecture produce measurable outcomes even without CBT/ERP
- Regulatory pathway de-risked: MHRA engagement uses a lower-risk feature set, increasing likelihood of favourable classification
- Q4 2026 beta timeline preserved for Phase 1
- AI airlock exploration could provide regulatory clarity faster than traditional classification pathway
- Constructive relationship with MHRA established before the 2026 regulatory framework crystallises
- Phase 1 private beta with Living with Limerence community validates product-market fit before Phase 2 investment

**Bad:**
- Core clinical features deferred: FR-008 (ABCDE cognitive restructuring) and FR-012 (guided ERP) are the primary intervention mechanisms from the Wyant (2021) clinical case study
- Longer total timeline to full feature set -- Phase 2 timeline depends on MHRA decision speed, which is outside project control
- Phase 1 tool may be perceived as insufficient by users expecting therapeutic intervention at the point of crisis
- Risk R-002 residual score remains at 16 (High) -- phased approach limits blast radius but does not reduce classification likelihood

**Neutral:**
- Phase assessment (FR-003) retained in Phase 1 but requires careful positioning as "response calibration" rather than clinical triage
- Anti-dependency architecture is fully operational in Phase 1, supporting validation of the tool's core differentiator (Goal G-4) independently of the regulatory decision
- The phased approach creates a natural experiment: Phase 1 outcome data demonstrates whether psychoeducation and ritual tracking alone produce measurable ritual reduction (Outcome O-1)

## Pros and Cons of the Options

### Option 1: Do Nothing -- Launch with Full CBT/ERP Features Without MHRA Engagement

Launch the complete feature set (psychoeducation, ritual tracking, ABCDE cognitive restructuring, guided ERP, phase assessment) without seeking MHRA classification determination.

| Criterion | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Regulatory risk | Very High -- risk of forced withdrawal if classified as medical device post-launch |
| Timeline | Fast -- no regulatory delay for initial launch |
| User value | Full clinical feature set available from day 1 |
| Cost | Low upfront -- but potentially catastrophic if enforcement action taken |
| Stakeholder trust | Destroyed if forced withdrawal occurs; MHRA relationship adversarial |

* Good, because users get immediate access to full clinical toolkit
* Good, because fastest path to market
* Bad, because regulatory non-compliance risk is unacceptable for a tool targeting vulnerable users
* Bad, because forced withdrawal would harm users already engaged with the tool
* Bad, because destroys stakeholder trust -- Clinical Advisory Board, MHRA, and users
* Bad, because sets adversarial tone with MHRA before the 2026 regulatory framework is published
* Bad, because directly conflicts with BR-004 (regulatory compliance before launch) and Goal G-3

### Option 2: Phased Launch Strategy with Early MHRA Engagement (Chosen)

Phase 1 (beta): Psychoeducation, ritual tracking, anti-dependency architecture -- clearly below device threshold. Phase 2: Add ABCDE cognitive restructuring and guided ERP once MHRA classification resolved. Engage MHRA via pre-submission meeting using Phase 1 scope. Explore AI airlock (regulatory sandbox).

| Criterion | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Regulatory risk | Low for Phase 1; managed for Phase 2 via MHRA engagement |
| Timeline | Phase 1 on schedule (Q4 2026); Phase 2 dependent on MHRA |
| User value | Genuine but partial -- psychoeducation and ritual tracking deliver measurable value |
| Cost | Low -- no regulatory costs for Phase 1; Phase 2 costs informed by MHRA determination |
| Stakeholder trust | Strong -- proactive regulatory engagement, clinical features deferred responsibly |

* Good, because de-risks regulatory pathway with lower-risk Phase 1 scope
* Good, because Phase 1 still delivers genuine user value (ritual tracking, psychoeducation, anti-dependency architecture)
* Good, because MHRA engagement with Phase 1 scope more likely to receive favourable classification
* Good, because AI airlock provides structured pathway for regulatory clarity
* Good, because preserves Q4 2026 beta timeline
* Good, because Phase 1 outcome data strengthens Phase 2 evidence base
* Bad, because defers core clinical features (FR-008 ABCDE, FR-012 ERP) to Phase 2
* Bad, because total timeline to full feature set is longer than Option 1 or Option 3
* Bad, because Phase 2 timeline depends on MHRA decision speed (external dependency)

### Option 3: Full Launch with Medical Device Pathway

Accept the likely Class IIa classification and pursue the full medical device regulatory pathway: UKCA marking, Notified Body assessment, clinical evidence generation, quality management system (ISO 13485), post-market surveillance.

| Criterion | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Regulatory risk | None -- full compliance achieved |
| Timeline | 12-18 month delay beyond current schedule |
| User value | Full clinical feature set available at launch |
| Cost | £50,000-100,000+ regulatory costs; ISO 13485 QMS implementation |
| Stakeholder trust | High regulatory credibility; but users wait 12-18 months longer |

* Good, because full clinical feature set from day 1 of (delayed) launch
* Good, because regulatory certainty -- no ambiguity about classification
* Good, because UKCA marking provides clinical credibility with mental health professionals
* Bad, because 12-18 month delay conflicts with Q4 2026 beta target (Goal G-1)
* Bad, because requires Notified Body assessment -- limited UK capacity post-Brexit
* Bad, because £50,000-100,000+ regulatory costs are disproportionate for founder-led project (£275K 3-year TCO)
* Bad, because requires clinical trial evidence before deployment -- no existing evidence base for limerence-specific digital interventions
* Bad, because ISO 13485 quality management system overhead for a small team
* Bad, because users in crisis cannot access the tool for an additional 12-18 months

### Option 4: Position as Wellness Tool Only

Strip all CBT/ERP features entirely. Provide only psychoeducation content, general wellbeing information, and community signposting. No ritual tracking, no phase assessment, no cognitive restructuring, no ERP.

| Criterion | Assessment |
|-----------|------------|
| Regulatory risk | None -- clearly not a medical device |
| Timeline | Fast -- no regulatory concerns |
| User value | Minimal -- tool is essentially a curated information resource |
| Cost | Low |
| Stakeholder trust | Low -- Clinical Advisory Board and users see no clinical value |

* Good, because clearly not a medical device -- no MHRA risk whatsoever
* Good, because fastest and cheapest path to market
* Bad, because removes the clinical value proposition entirely
* Bad, because the tool becomes a blog with a chatbot -- does not address BR-001 (deliver limerence recovery tool)
* Bad, because does not operationalise the Wyant (2021) clinical framework
* Bad, because anti-dependency architecture has nothing meaningful to constrain
* Bad, because no measurable clinical outcomes possible (Outcome O-1 unachievable)
* Bad, because Clinical Advisory Board has no reason to participate
